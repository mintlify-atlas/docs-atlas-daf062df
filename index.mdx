# Tool-Call Mechanistic Interpretability

This project investigates a fundamental question in language model agents: **when a model decides to call a tool versus answer directly, what's actually happening inside the network?**

Large language models are increasingly used as agents that can invoke external tools (calculators, search engines, APIs). But we have no mechanistic understanding of *how* the model makes that binary decision. This experiment uses interpretability techniques from the circuits literature — activation patching, direct logit attribution (DLA), and sparse autoencoder (SAE) feature analysis — to reverse-engineer the "tool decision circuit" inside a fine-tuned GPT-2 Small (124M parameters).

## The Research Question

**Primary question:** How do transformer circuits decide when to call a tool versus answer directly? Can we identify specific attention heads or SAE features that drive tool-invocation decisions?

**Secondary question:** Are the circuits identified under standard datasets robust to adversarial confound removal, or do they partially reflect surface-feature shortcuts?

## What We Found

Through a series of interpretability experiments on a fine-tuned GPT-2 Small model, we discovered:

<Info>
**Key Finding**: With adversarially-balanced data (removing number/keyword shortcuts), the tool-calling decision is **highly distributed and redundant** across attention heads. No small subset of heads is both sufficient and necessary.
</Info>

### Core Results

1. **Distributed Circuit**: Top 3 heads by activation patching (L7H6, L2H1, L6H2) are within 8% of each other — no single "tool decision head" dominates

2. **Two-Stage Pattern**: Early/mid-network heads (L2-L7) compute intermediate features; late-network heads (L9-L11) read and amplify the signal

3. **Surface Shortcuts**: In the original analysis, L6H4 dominated by detecting numbers. When numbers were balanced across classes, L6H4 became irrelevant (score ≈ 0)

4. **Circuit Ablation**:
   - **Sufficiency**: 50% accuracy (chance level) when keeping only 15 circuit heads
   - **Necessity**: 100% accuracy when removing those 15 heads
   - Interpretation: Behavior is highly redundant

5. **Unified Tool Circuit**: 8-10/10 overlap in top heads across all tool types (calculator, lookup, datetime, unit_conversion)

6. **SAE Features**: Top 20 features by Cohen's d (effect sizes -12 to -19) are all no-tool-favoring, but show near-zero individual causal effect when patched

## The Dataset

A critical design choice: our dataset is **adversarially balanced** to eliminate surface-feature shortcuts:

| Confound | Tool-call | No-tool | Purpose |
|----------|-----------|---------|----------|
| **Numbers present** | 59.9% | 59.4% | Prevents "numbers → tool" shortcut |
| **Dynamic-cue words** ("current", "today") | 34.3% | 29.4% | Prevents "temporal keyword → tool" shortcut |

**Dataset statistics:**
- 10,000 examples (5,000 tool-call, 5,000 no-tool)
- 4 tool types: calculator, lookup, datetime, unit_conversion
- 80/10/10 train/val/test split
- Max length: 256 tokens
- Deterministic generation (seed=42)

## Model Performance

| Metric | Value |

## Limitations

<Warning>
This is a **toy research setting**: GPT-2 Small (124M params) on a synthetic dataset with 99.5% accuracy. Real tool-calling in frontier models involves multi-step reasoning, tool selection, and argument construction. Findings may not generalize to larger models or naturalistic prompts.
</Warning>

Key caveats:
- Aggressive zero-ablation (more destructive than mean-ablation)
- Pretrained SAEs (not trained on our fine-tuned model)
- All examples follow same template structure
- Limited seed/checkpoint coverage
- GPU-targeted but can run on CPU (slower)
